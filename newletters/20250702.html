Hello all!

Things have been mostly1 smooth sailing since my last email!



By itself, that would be a pretty boring update. However, I recently realized that it’s been a little over a year since I started expanding Hyperdrive to users outside my home. As is my wont, I did some data analysis as a little treat to myself. That’s how everybody celebrates, right?

Plex and Overseerr Jellyseerr both keep data in a SQLite database, so I ran some queries2 and pulled out some statistics about the past year-ish that we can all enjoy together.

Total Play-Time vs. User Count

Since launch we’ve streamed the equivalent of ≈150 days of media, and the curve is not only increasing, it’s accelerating. Even during stretches when I added no new users, the slope kept steepening, which means the existing crowd is watching more per household. Every new person who joins quickly boosts the curve. We added roughly one “watch-day” every eight days last spring; now it’s one every 1.7 days.

Heatmap of Playback (Time of Day × Day of Week)

A heatmap of 5,496 play events shows that Hyperdrive’s rush hour is 8:00 PM to 11:00 PM. Not surprising, but fun to visualize. Weekdays 3:00 AM to 7:00 AM remain almost unused, which is good to know if I need a maintenance window.

TV vs Movie Share (by Duration)

Measured by minutes, 60% of all viewing time goes to episodic TV and 39% goes to movies. The remaining 1% belongs to a small library of high-quality music that I keep for myself.

Transcode Decision by Device

Before the chart, a brief explanation: Transcoding is Hyperdrive’s real-time process of converting a video or audio stream into a format that the client (Apple TV, Roku, iOS, etc.) can play when it can’t handle the original file directly. At the time of playback, one of three transcode decisions can be made:

  • Direct Play means that the file is already in a format your device understands (video codec, audio codec, resolution). Hyperdrive just sends the bits as-is. Fastest, zero hardware effort, best quality.
  • Direct Stream means that the client can handle video track but something small (usually container or subtitle track) isn’t. Hyperdrive remuxes on-the-fly — swaps the wrapper or burns-in the subtitles — while leaving the video untouched. Light hardware use; quality unchanged.
  • Transcode means that the client can’t handle the original video or audio codec (or the bitrate or resolution is too high). Hyperdrive converts the stream into a compatible format in real time: shrinks resolution, re-encodes audio, etc. Highest hardware load; slight quality drop but ensures playback on any screen.

Apple TV and iOS make up 75% of all streams (by play count), which is good news, because they only transcode 8% of the time. Roku, on the other hand, transcodes 83% of its sessions. Lastly, HTML5 players have stricter codec requirements, so web browsers end up transcoding 88% of their sessions. The numbers don’t lie: if you’re gonna stream from Hyperdrive, you’ll have the best experience doing so from an app, not a browser.

Cumulative Jellyseerr Requests

We’re averaging 4.8 requests per day right now, compared to just 1.2 a year ago. Every month this year has set a new record number of requests: from 47 in January to 137 in June. We hit 1,000 Jellyseerr requests on June 18th. Before charting the data, I naively assumed that cumulative request growth was linear, and we’d hit 2,000 after another year or so. This is not the case. Based on current trends, we’re actually due to hit 2,000 requests as soon as December 19th of this year.

Thanks to everyone for providing me with a hobby, and a special thank you to anyone who has reported any issues with content. If the data above proves anything, it’s that I can’t QA every piece of media that comes in.

Happy streaming!
